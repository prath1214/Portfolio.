    Projects

  • Title - Comparative Study of EV Battery Cooling System Using Liquid Flow-Channel
  • Project Advisor - Dr. Mahesh Joshi
    • This study evaluates a liquid cooling system where coolant flows through channels integrated near battery cells to dissipate operational heat. Simulations were conducted using water and ethylene glycol across three cooling channel designs: serpentine, wavy, and embedded. Results show water outperforms ethylene glycol in all configurations, with the highest heat transfer rate of 39.12% observed in the serpentine channel, followed by 31.35% in the wavy channel, and 5.09% in the embedded design.

  • Project Advisor - Mr. Sudesh Powar
    • This project presents a sustainable refrigeration system utilizing thermoelectric cooling via a Peltier module, eliminating the need for traditional compressor-based mechanisms. Powered by a solar PV panel, the system converts electrical energy into a temperature gradient, enabling cooling without harmful refrigerants. The setup includes a heat sink, fan, and insulation chamber to enhance heat dissipation and cooling efficiency. The system demonstrates effective cooling in low-load applications with reduced noise, maintenance, and environmental impactâ€”making it suitable for small-scale, off-grid refrigeration needs.
  • Title - Solar Still
  • Project Advisor - Mr. Sudesh Powar

    • A solar still is a passive desalination device that uses solar energy to purify water through evaporation and condensation. Saline or contaminated water is placed in a basin and heated by solar radiation through a transparent cover. The water evaporates, leaving impurities behind, and condenses on the cooler inner surface of the cover, where it is collected as distilled water. Solar stills are simple, energy-efficient, and suitable for remote or arid regions with limited access to clean water.

  • Project Advisor - Dr. Mahesh Joshi
  • Problem Statement - Regenerative braking in EVs recovers energy lost during braking, improving efficiency. Current systems lack optimal performance, requiring better design and control.
  • Introduction - Regenerative braking in electric vehicles (EVs) converts kinetic energy during deceleration into electrical energy, which is stored in the battery, improving overall energy efficiency. This case study analyzes the performance of regenerative braking integrated with the EVâ€™s motor controller and battery management system. Key parameters such as braking torque, energy recovery rate, and battery SOC (state of charge) are evaluated. Results indicate that regenerative braking can recover up to 30% of energy during urban driving, extending range and reducing mechanical brake wear, making it essential for efficient EV operation.
  • Conclusion - Regenerative braking significantly enhances the efficiency of electric vehicles by recovering energy during deceleration. An optimized system can reduce energy loss, extend battery life, and improve overall vehicle performance. Continued development and integration of advanced control strategies are essential for maximizing its benefits.
